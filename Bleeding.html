Bleeding

Immediate Care and First Aid Steps

Overview

​

Bleeding is the loss of blood from the circulatory system, usually caused by injury to blood vessels. It can range from minor cuts and scrapes to severe, life-threatening hemorrhage. While small wounds often stop bleeding on their own or with simple first aid, major bleeding requires immediate medical attention to prevent shock or death

When to Seek Emergency Care

When to Seek Emergency Care Call 911 (or your local emergency number) immediately if bleeding:

  • Is severe and does not stop after 10 minutes of firm pressure.
  • Spurts or gushes from the wound (suggesting arterial bleeding).
  • Is accompanied by signs of shock (pale skin, rapid breathing, weak pulse).
  • Occurs after a major accident, fall, or trauma.
  • Involves deep wounds, especially to the chest, abdomen, or head.
  • Is internal (vomiting blood, coughing blood, blood in urine/stool).
⚠️ Note: Bleeding in infants, elderly, or people with clotting disorders (like hemophilia) requires urgent care even if it seems minor.

Treatment

Major Bleeding (Emergency Response)

While waiting for emergency medical teams, provide the following first aid:

  • Apply pressure: Use a clean cloth or sterile gauze to press firmly on the wound.
  • Do not remove soaked cloths:Place additional layers on top instead.
  • Elevate the wound: If possible, raise the injured area above heart level.
  • Use a tourniquet (last resort): Only if bleeding is uncontrollable and professional help is delayed.
  • Monitor breathing and consciousness:Be ready to perform CPR if necessary.
  • Prevent shock: Lay the person down, keep them warm, and reassure them.

Minor Bleeding (Cuts and Scrapes)

For minor bleeding, follow these steps:

  • Clean the wound: Rinse gently with clean water to remove dirt.
  • Apply pressure: Use a clean cloth until bleeding stops.
  • Disinfect: Apply antiseptic solution or ointment to prevent infection.
  • Bandage: Cover with a sterile bandage or plaster.
  • Pain relief:Use over-the-counter relievers if needed.

What to Avoid (Precautions)

  • ❌ Do not wash severe wounds with strong pressure (may worsen bleeding).
  • ❌ Do not remove large or deeply embedded objects—leave them for professionals.
  • ❌ Do not apply a tourniquet unless absolutely necessary.
  • ❌ Do not ignore persistent or unexplained bleeding.

When to Contact a Doctor

  • If bleeding continues despite first aid.
  • If the wound shows signs of infection (redness, swelling, pus).
  • If you haven’t had a tetanus shot in the last 5 years and the wound is deep.
  • If you have a medical condition affecting blood clotting.
